Why This Direction

Public feedback points to a mobile execution gap, not a demand gap.

Users already want HoneyBook to be their operating system. The opportunity is to remove friction from the exact phone flows that keep bookings, proposals, and payments moving.

Pain 01

Speed and stability still get in the way

Recent App Store and Play feedback calls out slow screens, crashes, glitches, and lag during core actions. When the phone stops feeling dependable, users bounce back to desktop or stop acting in the moment.

Pain 02

Mobile completion is still inconsistent

Public reviews specifically mention editing gaps, invoice friction, weak iPad behavior, and proposal workflows that do not feel fully finishable on mobile. That is support debt, not just UX polish work.

Pain 03

AI now has to earn trust in the core flow

HoneyBook is leaning harder into AI follow-ups, meeting notes, and suggestions. That only compounds value if the mobile app surfaces the right action instantly and keeps the rest of the experience calm.

Execution Lens

A tighter mobile surface can support both product velocity and support efficiency.

This is intentionally framed as an engineering-friendly improvement: compress the path to action, expose small inline edits where they matter most, and make AI feel embedded instead of bolted on.

What changes in the product shape

Lead Pulse becomes the mobile entry point instead of a broad dashboard.
Silent leads, proposal risk, and payment blockers are ranked by urgency.
AI creates the next action only when it removes typing, search, or context switching.

Why engineering should care

It reduces the number of mobile surfaces users need to trust. Fewer screen transitions and fewer dead-end flows means less room for lag, state bugs, and frustrated support tickets.

Why the business should care

HoneyBook already owns the workflow. Making mobile action feel instantaneous is how that workflow turns into higher adoption, fewer workarounds, and stronger belief in the AI roadmap.
